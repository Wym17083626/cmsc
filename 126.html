Stanford Corners the 'Smart' Market

After Its Best Football Season in Years, School Chases Top Recruits With Elite Grades; Building Robots

By DARREN EVERSON AND JARED DIAMOND

View Full Image

Getty Images

Stanford's football team gets ready to run onto the field before the Orange Bowl on Jan. 3.

As college football's 2011 recruiting classes took shape last week, much of the talk was dominated by the usual question: Which team pulled in the richest talent haul? Some say it was Alabama, others Florida State.

What was not acknowledged, or even noted, was the impressive and unusual incoming class assembled by Stanford.

The school, which is coming off its best football season in 70 years, didn't land the most physically talented class of high school football players. The consensus says their crop ranks somewhere around No. 20 in the nation among all the major college programs. What stands out about Stanford's class is something entirely different: what superior students they are.

Wayne Lyons, a four-star defensive back from Fort Lauderdale, Fla., who has a 4.96 weighted grade-point average and likes to build robots in his spare time, is widely considered the best student among the nation's elite recruits. When he visited Stanford, he said he was whisked to a seminar on building jet engines and to a facility where robots are built.

View Full Image

Associated Press

Linebacker recruit James Vaughters, a 4.0 student from Georgia.

"Stanford made the academics a huge priority," said Mr. Lyons, who considered Notre Dame, Florida and Auburn, among others. "The only place that came close was Michigan."

James Vaughters, a four-star linebacker and 4.0 student from Stone Mountain, Ga., who wants to study architecture, said he picked Stanford "when someone told me, 'If you get hurt at so-and-so school, would you still want to be there, or would you want to be somewhere else?' The only place I could say that about was Stanford."

Each year, there are more than 1,100 high school recruits who receive scouting grades of three stars to five stars (on a five-star scale). These players are the ones who are the most coveted by schools and who tend to end up playing for the top programs.

In interviews, several top high school football scouts, talent evaluators and coaches identified about a dozen blue-chip recruits who—based on their reported grades, test scores and extracurricular activities—are also elite students. A significant number of these kids have one thing in common: Stanford pursued them aggressively. In the end, the scouts said, four of them decided to enroll there.

During college football's recruiting season, which culminated last week with signing day, the topic of academics is rarely even broached. Recruiting services don't even bother assessing which players are the best student-athletes. In most cases, the only time academics enter into the equation is when there's a chance the student's grades and test scores may be too low to make him eligible.

"Ninety-nine percent of people gave up the farce of the student-athlete long, long ago," said Scott Kennedy, the director of scouting at Scout.com, a recruiting site. "It's a business. It's great that these kids are so smart and great football players, but no one is paying to watch them debate. They're watching them play football."

After years of football mediocrity, Stanford emerged last year as a national powerhouse under then-coach Jim Harbaugh. The team went 12-1 and finished the season by stomping Virginia Tech in the Orange Bowl and earning the No. 4 ranking. Quarterback Andrew Luck, who finished second in the Heisman Trophy balloting, is among the leading candidates to win the award next season.

The team's rise was led, in large part, by players who were also good students. Mr. Luck is an architectural design major with a reported 3.5 GPA. In the previous season, when Stanford improved to 8-5, the team rallied around running back Toby Gerhart, who finished second in the Heisman voting. He was a management science and engineering major with a reported 3.25 GPA.

"The guys Stanford is recruiting are good enough in math to know their chances of sticking in the NFL versus the value of a $150,000 education," said Mr. Kennedy. "They now have a chance to get that great education and win big."

If the team can sustain this success in coming seasons, it could give Stanford enormous bragging rights at a time when college football has never seemed more of a business. Elite academic schools have had their moments in the past. Northwestern reached the Rose Bowl following the 1995 season and has remained mostly respectable. But Stanford's prospects seem stronger. This year's incoming class is Stanford's third consecutive group that ranked in the top 25.

Granted, this could fall apart at any time. The Cardinal's football fortunes may change once Mr. Luck leaves school. The program also may not have nearly as much recruiting success without Mr. Harbaugh, who has moved on to the NFL's San Francisco 49ers. He was replaced by the team's offensive coordinator, David Shaw. Four recruits who were headed to Stanford decided to go elsewhere after the coach defected, leaving the school with a class of 19.

Stanford also must recruit from a smaller pool—a fact that analysts say could slow them down in the future. "One of the coaches told me they can only recruit from 250 kids across the country that are academically eligible, and even fewer of those are premier Division I athletes," said Stanford signee Remound Wright, a four-star running back from Fort Wayne, Ind. with a 4.42 GPA. Mr. Shaw couldn't be reached for comment.

For now, however, Stanford is using its success on the field—and its elite academics—as a powerful combination to lure players. "You normally had to choose: Do I want to play in the NFL or do I want a good education?" said Mr. Wright. "Stanford is the one place you don't have to choose."
